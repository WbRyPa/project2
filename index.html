Describing the role of IT in 'my' work

From a requirement in the office to a staple of everyday life; in a nutshell that has been the transformation of IT in my work-life. IT is a vital cog in every area of industry and, as the global pandemic has shown, IT has now completely crossed the divisions of work-life and home-life.

Home-life is a key change for the role of IT, the two for many years were kept isolate from one another to prevent one encroaching on the other (the adage of work-life-balance). Today we use the same technology for work and to keep our vital bonds with friends and family, that we may not be able to see for numerous reasons (not just the Covid-19 pandemic).

For IT professionals, age demographics are no longer as important because the same technologies are now used by young an old in the same manner. This has posed multiple challenges as target audiences have now merged into a large community and the special interest groups are now being included in topical conversations on new levels. The same problems are seen by young and old alike and as a result new measures are required to protect a wider, and more vulnerable groups.

From research to communications, recreation to collaboration; IT is now essential for all aspects of my digital life.

Risks that might impact digital projects

Scope is an integral part of any digital project. If there is no direction or outcome clearly defined, then there are more opportunities to digress. New ideas can arrive and force a back-track, a new skill, or a new requirement can be rushed through to fit a timeline that has just a start and an end.

Expectations can be a major issue, following on from the previous point. The stakeholder or client may have requirements that are beyond the skillset of the developers, this can add time and expense as the developer needs to acquire the skill, or a third-party would need to be brought-in for a specific task. If this is not addressed at the start of the project then there could be issues with compatibility; after-all ‘you don’t know what you don’t know’ in this instance.
